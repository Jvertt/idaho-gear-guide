The Black Diamond Distance Z trekking poles solve a specific problem that North Idaho hikers face regularly: trails that alternate between terrain where poles are essential and terrain where they're in the way. The Z-fold design collapses to 15 inches in seconds, letting you stash them on your pack for scramble sections and redeploy for descents without stopping.

## Design and Packability

The three-section Z-fold design uses internal speed cone connections — pull the sections apart, fold, and the elastic cord collapses them into a compact bundle. Deployment is equally fast: unfold, snap the sections together, and the speed cones lock automatically. We timed the fold-to-deploy cycle at about 8 seconds — fast enough to do mid-stride on a transitioning trail.

At 15 inches collapsed, the poles fit inside most pack lids or strap to the side of a 30L daypack without catching on brush. For rock climbing approaches at Q'emiln Park or scramble routes on the Selkirk Crest, this packability is a genuine advantage over telescoping poles that collapse to 24-26 inches.

## Performance on Trail

The 7075 aluminum shafts are light but not flimsy. On sustained descents like the 1,400-foot drop from the Mineral Ridge summit back to Beauty Bay, the poles absorbed impact reliably and the EVA foam grips stayed comfortable. The foam extends below the grip for choking down on traverses — a thoughtful detail for the sidehill trails common in the Panhandle National Forest.

The FlickLock adjustability at the top section gives you about 7 inches of length tuning. This matters because the lower two sections are fixed-length, so you need to buy the correct size (Black Diamond offers four sizes based on height). The FlickLock compensates for minor adjustments — slightly longer for descents, shorter for ascents — and it's operated one-handed with gloves.

## Trail Testing

**Tubbs Hill Loop (casual):** Overkill for this trail, but the ability to fold and clip to a small daypack when descending the steeper east side section was convenient.

**Mineral Ridge (moderate):** Excellent on the 700-foot ascent and descent. The poles made the rocky, rooted descent significantly more comfortable on the knees.

**Selkirk Crest Ridge Route (strenuous):** The poles excelled here — deployed for ridge walking, folded for Class 2 scramble sections, and redeployed for the long descent. The transition speed meant we never lost momentum.

**Canfield Mountain (mountain biking access):** Folded and strapped to a backpack for the pedal up, deployed for the hiking portion of a bike-and-hike combo. The 10-oz weight was barely noticeable on the ride up.

## Limitations

The fixed-length lower sections mean you can't dial in a perfect length for extreme uphills or downhills the way you can with fully adjustable telescoping poles. The FlickLock upper section helps, but it's not a full substitute.

The carbide tips wear faster on rock than I'd like. After 100+ miles of rocky North Idaho trails, the tips are noticeably shorter. Replacement tips are available and inexpensive, but it's a recurring cost that telescoping poles with larger baskets and tips don't have.

The poles also aren't designed for heavy leaning — snow travel, river crossings, or supporting significant body weight on steep terrain. For those uses, a telescoping pole like the LEKI Makalu FX Carbon provides more rigidity and adjustability.

## Who Should Buy These Poles

The Distance Z poles are ideal for fast-and-light hikers, trail runners who want poles for descents, and anyone who hikes varied terrain where poles are sometimes helpful and sometimes in the way. If you always want poles deployed and you want maximum adjustability and durability, go telescoping. But for the typical North Idaho day hiker covering moderate-to-technical terrain, the Distance Z offers the best balance of weight, packability, and performance.
